   CONCEPTS AND MISCONCEPTIONS



Concepts are the building blocks of the knowledge structure in any discipline or subject. Conceptual clarity is, therefore, necessary in the subject to develop a proper understanding of the principles and theories of the subject. However, students often develop certain misconceptions related to a particular concept that hinder their learning in that subject. Identification of the misconceptions and its remediation is therefore an essential aspect of any instructional design.

This unit will help you

  • Understand the meaning of concepts, misconceptions and the difference between the two .

  • Identify the essential characteristics of a misconception.

  • Can present examples of misconceptions from their science lessons.

  • Understand the significance of knowing students’ misconceptions.



CONCEPT

According to Merriam-Webster Dictionary, a concept is “an abstract or generic idea generalized from particular instances”(Merriam-Webster, 2023). Concepts are, thus, mental representations or abstractions formed through generalization and that helps in communication (Langland-Hassan, et.al., 2022).

For example, Force is a common concept in physics. Force is an abstraction or a mental representation. This mental representation is constructed from various instances of pull and push. Thus whenever the term is used it denotes some sort of pull or push which is commonly understood by all those communicating in science.





DEFINING CHARACTERISTICS OF CONCEPT


There are certain important points about concepts that we need to understand before we try to see what concepts are:

  • First of all, we should understand that concept is an idea where an idea refers to some impression or some thought.

  • The idea is constructed through abstraction from several instances. This construction of abstraction is sort of mental representation.

  • The mental representation or the abstraction is given a name. Thus the term Force is the name given to the mental representation of push or pull.




  • What are these instances that you identified? These instances are examples of the concept. Thus every concept has a number of examples.

  • Since a concept has several instances/examples, a concept is qualified as generic in nature. The idea is generic because it represents a whole range of entities that are similar to each other rather than any particular entity. For example, Fruit is a concept. It is an impression for a wide range of edible objects such as bananas, apples, etc. rather than some one particular element.

  • It is a generalized idea because it is constructed based on the identification of the similarities in different instances of the concept. For example, Heat is a concept in science metalanguage. There is nothing concrete that we denote as Heat. Rather, scientists constructed the term and use it in a generalized way to denote several instances of a phenomenon involving hotness or coldness of the bodies.


  • Finally, the concept represents the building block of scientific knowledge. It is in terms of these concepts that more complex principles and theories in the discipline are understood and communicated.

MISCONCEPTION

Misconception is defined as a view that is considered to be inaccurate since it is based on erroneous or faulty thinking about some concept.
It is popularly referred to the inaccurate or faulty or erroneous ideas in students' knowledge structure that do not match with the accepted view of the science community about some concept.




ILLUSTRATION

Misconceptions are errors in students’ knowledge structure related to the target concept.




The idea of a misconception can be illustratively comprehended using the following examples:

  • A popular example of misconception quoted quite often is the students saying that the sun rises in the east and sets in the west which connotes the faulty conception of the solar system (National Academy Press).

  • A group of undergraduate chemistry students were asked to draw sketches of two bottles- A. One filled with air and B. Second from which half the air has been removed.
    Fifteen per cent of the students sketched half the second bottle showing the presence of air and the other half was shown to be empty (Benson, et, al, 1993).
  • Students were asked to find which of the two metallic objects of different sizes dropped from the same height in a vacuum will research ground earlier. Many students interpreted the heavier object to reach the ground first(Akhilesh, 2014).

CHARACTERISTICS OF MISCONCEPTIONS

Any misconception has three important characteristics.


Any misconception has three important characteristics.

  • Misconceptions differ from the valid accepted concept in science.

  • The misconceptions are consistent across the groups i.e. several students are holding the same misconception.

  • Misconceptions are resistant to change following the conventional instructional strategy (Champagne, Klopfer and Gunstone, 1982).

MISCONCEPTIONS AND LEARNING

The students have their own cognitive/knowledge structure related to some learned concept.

The knowledge structure [ in a correct way or as a misconception] constitutes the previous or the existing knowledge structure of the learner.

The students are said to interpret new learning material through the lens of their existing knowledge structure. Therefore, the previous knowledge, [including the misconceptions] mediates the students’ learning of new materials involving the particular target concept.

Since, the students, when faced with new learning material, interpret it via their existing knowledge structure which includes the misconceptions as well, the new learning is apt to be erroneous since it is interpreted through the lens of misconception.

Thus the faulty conceptions in the previous knowledge structure (i.e. misconception) are bound to interfere with the student’s future learning in a negative way that can lead to further erroneous learning.

In the worst case, the misconceptions make it hard for the students to learn the new material and as such the students get demotivated and give up learning.

MISCONCEPTIONS AND TEACHERS’ ROLE

It is widely accepted that

“Teachers who know their students' most common misconceptions are more likely to increase their students’ science knowledge than teachers who do not” (Sadler and Sonnert, 2016)


Therefore the teachers need to have an idea of their students’ misconceptions.
The knowledge of students’ misconceptions helps the teacher to intervene in their learning, leading them towards conceptual clarity.
Therefore, a concerned teacher will always try to assess the students for any existing misconceptions in their class and remedy the same.

The teachers have to significant roles concerning misconceptions. The two roles are:
  • Identification of the misconceptions.
  • Remedy the misconception through alternative instructional design.

SUMMARY

  • A concept is “an abstract or generic idea generalized from particular instances”(Webster, 2023).

  • Concepts are the building block of organized knowledge in science.

  • An error in understanding of a concept different from that accepted by the scientific community as valid is known as Misconception.

  • Misconceptions are invalid elements in the knowledge structure, that are systematic errors and that are difficult to remove by conventional teaching approaches.

  • Misconceptions hinder students' learning and may lead to errors in the future learning of students.

  • Teachers need to identify and remedy the students’ misconceptions early in life.
